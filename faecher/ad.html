Where is the aerodynamic centre of a lifting surface located?

  • At about 25 % of the chord in subsonic flow The classic quarter-chord position.
  • At about 40 … 50 % of the chord in supersonic flow The a.c. moves aft with supersonic flow — this rearward shift is a major source of trim drag for supersonic aircraft.
  • For a wing or stabilizer, on the mean aerodynamic chord at the same relative position as on the airfoil This is exactly why the MAC is constructed: it lets 2D airfoil knowledge be transferred to the 3D surface.
  • Always at the centre of gravity of the aircraft
  • It moves forward to about 10 % chord at supersonic speed

Careful: the formulas commonly used compute the mean geometric chord (MGC), which is only an approximation of the true MAC — the real MAC also depends on airfoil, twist and sweep.

Which aspect ratio band fits which aircraft category?

  • Low aspect ratio → fighters Low A gives high structural strength and high roll manoeuvrability — at the price of high induced drag.
  • Medium aspect ratio → general aviation and short-range transports A compromise between structural weight and drag.
  • High aspect ratio → long-range transports and sailplanes High A minimises induced drag and therefore maximises L/D — the decisive parameter for range and glide performance.
  • Very low aspect ratio → sailplanes

Remember the geometric coupling: for a fixed wing area, b = √(A · Sref) — a higher aspect ratio directly means a larger span.

Which quantities are plotted on the axes of a matching diagram (constraint diagram)?

  • Wing loading W/S on the horizontal axis W/S is the first of the two sizing outputs — it fixes the wing area once the take-off mass is known.
  • Thrust-to-weight ratio T/W (or power loading W/P) on the vertical axis The second sizing output — it fixes the required engine size.
  • Range over payload
  • Stabilizer area ratio over c.g. position

Each point performance requirement (take-off, landing, climb, cruise, ceiling, manoeuvre) is transformed into a curve in this plane. The feasible region is the intersection of all constraints; the design point is chosen inside it.

Which of the following requirements appear as an (almost) vertical line in the matching diagram?

  • Landing field length The approach/landing speed follows from W/S and CL,max,L only — thrust does not appear, so the constraint is a vertical limit on wing loading.
  • Maximum stall speed requirement Same reason: VS = √(2(W/S)/(ρ CL,max)) is independent of installed thrust.
  • Second-segment climb gradient with one engine inoperative
  • Take-off field length

Vertical constraints only limit W/S; horizontal constraints only limit T/W; all others couple both.

Landing normally sets the maximum wing loading, take-off and climb set the minimum thrust-to-weight ratio.

Where is the design point usually chosen in the matching diagram of a transport aircraft?

  • At the highest permissible wing loading and the lowest thrust-to-weight ratio that still satisfies all constraints A high W/S means a small wing (low weight, low wetted area, low drag) and a low T/W means a small engine (low weight, low fuel burn and low cost).
  • At the lowest possible wing loading
  • At the highest possible thrust-to-weight ratio
  • Anywhere outside the feasible region, as long as it is close to it

Practical designs often keep a small margin to the constraint boundaries, because the underlying assumptions (CL,max, CD0, masses) are still uncertain at this stage.

During wing design it turns out that the assumed CL,max,Landing cannot be achieved with the chosen high-lift system. What is the consequence for the sizing?

  • The landing constraint moves to a lower wing loading, so the wing has to become larger W/S at landing is proportional to CL,max,L. Less maximum lift means less permissible wing loading.
  • The preliminary sizing has to be repeated with the lower lift coefficients Exactly the feedback loop described in the lecture: sizing assumptions must be confirmed by the high-lift design, otherwise the loop is closed again.
  • The required take-off thrust may increase A reduced CL,max,T-O shifts the take-off constraint upwards, demanding more thrust for the same field length.
  • The wing can be made smaller

This is why Chapter 9 explicitly checks whether the high-lift system can deliver ΔCL,max = CL,max,T-O/L − CL,max,clean.

Breguet's range equation for a jet aircraft reads R = (V/c)·(L/D)·ln(W1/W2). Which measures increase the range?

  • Reducing the thrust specific fuel consumption c Range is inversely proportional to c — engine efficiency enters linearly.
  • Increasing the aerodynamic efficiency L/D L/D enters linearly, which is why drag reduction and high aspect ratios pay off directly in range.
  • Increasing the fuel mass fraction, i.e. the ratio W1/W2 More fuel relative to the landing weight increases the logarithm — but only logarithmically, so doubling the fuel does not double the range.
  • Increasing the operating empty weight at constant take-off weight
  • Increasing CD0

For a propeller aircraft the equivalent form is R = (ηP/cP) · (L/D) · ln(W1/W2) — the speed V drops out and the propeller efficiency takes its place.

Which flight condition gives the maximum range of a propeller-driven aircraft (Breguet, constant altitude)?

  • Flight at maximum L/D For a propeller the range equation contains L/D directly, so best range coincides with (L/D)max.
  • Flight at maximum CL3/2/CD
  • Flight at maximum CL1/2/CD
  • Flight at CL,max

Summary: propeller → best range at (L/D)max, best endurance at max CL3/2/CD. Jet → best range at max CL1/2/CD, best endurance at (L/D)max.

The designer increases the aspect ratio of the wing while keeping the wing area and the mission constant. Which effects on the sizing result are to be expected?

  • L/Dmax increases, so less fuel is needed for the same range Induced drag falls with 1/A, which raises the maximum aerodynamic efficiency.
  • The wing structural weight increases Larger span means larger bending moments and a heavier wing box — this counteracts the fuel saving.
  • A trade study is required to find the optimum, because the two effects work against each other This is the classical aspect ratio trade: aerodynamic gain versus structural penalty.
  • The lift curve slope CL,α decreases
  • The stall angle of attack increases

Effects of a reduced aspect ratio, for comparison: lower lift curve slope, higher stall angle of attack, lower CL,max, more induced drag, lighter structure.

Why is an accurate mass prediction so critical in the early design phase?

  • Mass directly drives aircraft size, engine thrust and therefore acquisition and operating cost The whole sizing loop is anchored on the take-off mass — an error propagates into every subsequent result.
  • Weight growth in late design stages severely degrades performance and is expensive to fix The Boeing 787 was about 8 % overweight in the early versions, which cost roughly 15 % of range.
  • The decision about the feasibility of a project is taken before the costly detail design starts A wrong mass estimate can lead to a project that is only recognised as unfeasible when large sums have already been spent — see the Boeing 2707 SST.
  • Mass distribution is irrelevant as long as the total mass is right

Component masses are needed to find the overall c.g., to place the wing and heavy systems, and as target values for the system designers.

What distinguishes Class I from Class II mass estimation methods?

  • Class I uses simple statistical fractions or factors derived from historical aircraft data Typically a component mass is expressed as a fixed percentage of MTOM or as a factor times a reference area.
  • Class II uses refined regression formulas including geometric and load parameters Class II formulas contain things like S, A, sweep, t/c, ultimate load factor and design speed.
  • Both are only valid for the aircraft class they were derived from Applying a transport-aircraft formula to a sailplane-like aspect ratio produces nonsense. The methods are not interchangeable.
  • Both methods are unit-independent
Always cross-check a mass estimate with a second method, and use real component masses whenever they are available.

Which parameters still have to be determined during wing design, after preliminary sizing has already delivered the wing area?

  • Sweep angle and taper ratio Both are planform parameters fixed in Chapter 8.
  • Airfoil, twist and incidence angle The "wing design" group of parameters, as opposed to the planform group.
  • Dihedral angle and the location and size of aileron and spoiler Dihedral drives lateral stability; aileron size drives roll control power and constrains the space available for trailing-edge flaps.
  • The wing reference area Sref, which is not known before

The aspect ratio has usually been estimated already in preliminary sizing (via the assumed L/Dmax), but it is revisited and iterated in wing design.

Which statements about the effect of the aspect ratio A are correct?

  • Increasing A reduces the induced drag CD,i = CL²/(π A e) — the induced drag falls inversely with A.
  • Increasing A increases the wing structural weight The larger span raises the root bending moment.
  • Reducing A increases the stall angle of attack Low aspect ratio wings stall late and gently — this is why stabilizers are given low aspect ratios.
  • Reducing A reduces CL,max Together with the reduced lift curve slope this is one reason why low-A wings need large angles of attack.
  • Reducing A increases the lift curve slope

A = b² / Sref   and   CD,i = CL² / (π A e)

What are the two legitimate reasons to sweep a wing?

  • To delay the onset of shock waves and thus the wave drag rise Sweep reduces the effective Mach number normal to the quarter chord: Maeff = Ma · cos(Λc/4).
  • To fix a centre-of-gravity problem Classic example: the DC-3 used sweep to cure an aft-c.g. problem; canard or aft-engine layouts may also need it.
  • To improve the aerodynamic efficiency at low speed
  • To improve the stall behaviour of an aft-swept wing
  • To reduce the wing structural weight
Regarding the critical Mach number it makes no difference whether the sweep is positive or negative — only |cosΛ| matters.

A wing has a quarter-chord sweep of 30° and an unswept maximum lift coefficient of 1.5. Which value should be used as a first approximation for the swept wing?

  • ≈ 1.3 cL,max,swept ≈ cL,max,unswept · cos(Λc/4) = 1.5 · cos30° ≈ 1.30.
  • 1.5, unchanged
  • ≈ 1.7
  • ≈ 1.1

Sweep also reduces the lift curve slope and shifts CL,max to a higher angle of attack.

Why do aft-swept wings tend to "pitch up" at the stall?

  • The tip stalls first, and the tip is located behind the c.g. Losing lift behind the c.g. removes a nose-down contribution.
  • The still-attached inboard wing forward of the c.g. keeps lifting and produces a nose-up moment The result is a strong nose-up moment that drives the aircraft deeper into the stall — a self-aggravating and dangerous behaviour.
  • Forward-swept wings are completely free of pitch-up
  • Forward-swept wings lose aileron control first

Counter-measures on aft-swept wings: wash-out, spanwise change of airfoil (aerodynamic twist), fences, vortilons, and a careful choice of taper ratio.

Which effects does a decreasing taper ratio λ = ctip/croot have?

  • The structural weight can be reduced The centre of pressure shifts inboard, reducing the wing bending moments.
  • The available fuel volume is reduced The outboard wing becomes thin and small, so tank volume is lost.
  • The lift and lift-coefficient distribution over the span changes, affecting induced drag and stall behaviour Taper is the main planform tool to approach an elliptical lift distribution — but too small a taper ratio triggers tip stall.
  • There is no practical lower limit for the taper ratio

Additional effect: the low local Reynolds number at a small tip chord further promotes the tendency towards tip stall.

Which statements about the airfoil thickness ratio t/c are correct?

  • Wing structural weight varies approximately inversely with the square root of t/c Wwing ~ 1/√(t/c). Halving the thickness ratio therefore increases the wing weight by about 41 %.
  • In subsonic conditions the maximum lift is usually achieved around t/c = 12 … 16 % Both thinner and much thicker airfoils give lower cl,max.
  • A larger t/c lowers the critical Mach number A thicker airfoil accelerates the flow more strongly, so sonic conditions are reached at a lower free-stream Mach number.
  • t/c influences the available volume for structure, fuel and landing gear A practical constraint that often dominates over the pure aerodynamic optimum.
  • t/c has practically no influence on drag

Which stall tendency belongs to which airfoil thickness class?

  • t/c > 14 % ("fat"): trailing edge separation, gradual loss of lift, small change of pitching moment The gentle behaviour comes at a price: control power in the flapped section is lost early.
  • t/c = 6 … 13 % ("normal"): leading edge separation, sudden loss of lift and sudden change of pitching moment Control power is however maintained up to higher angles of attack.
  • t/c < 6 % ("thin"): leading edge separation, gradual loss of lift but significant change of pitching moment Control power is also maintained to high α.
  • t/c < 6 %: trailing edge separation with a very gentle stall

For wings with low or medium aspect ratio and/or high sweep, the stall behaviour is dominated by twist and sweep — in that case the 2D stall type can be ignored during airfoil selection.

What does the designation NACA 23012 mean?

  • Design lift coefficient 0.3, maximum camber at 15 % chord, normal camber line, 12 % thick 5-digit series: 1st digit × 3/20 = design Cl; 2nd digit × 5 = camber position in %; 3rd digit 0 = normal, 1 = reflexed; last two digits = thickness in %.
  • 2 % camber at 30 % chord, 12 % thick
  • Laminar flow to 23 % chord, design Cl = 0.1, 2 % thick
  • A second-phase supercritical airfoil

The 5-digit series was designed for superior maximum lift and low pitching moments — but it has a harsh stall behaviour.

Match the airfoil designations to their meaning.

  • NACA 2414: 2 % camber at 40 % chord, 14 % thick 4-digit series — good stall behaviour, well suited for general aviation.
  • NACA 63-314: laminar flow over 30 % of the chord, design Cl = 0.3, 14 % thick 6-series — emphasis on laminar flow. The 2nd digit gives the minimum-pressure point in tens of percent.
  • SC(2)-0714: second-phase supercritical airfoil, design Cl = 0.7, 14 % thick NASA SC-series — good transonic behaviour while retaining decent low-speed characteristics.
  • NACA 651-412: the subscript 1 means 1 % thickness

An "A" after the second digit of a 6-series airfoil means the trailing edge cusp has been removed for easier manufacturing.

Which statements about wing twist are correct?

  • Geometric twist rotates the same airfoil section along the span αtip < αroot for wash-out.
  • Aerodynamic twist uses different airfoils along the span αzero-lift,tip < αzero-lift,root. In practice both types are usually combined.
  • Twist can be used to approach an elliptical lift distribution and thus minimum induced drag But only for the single angle of attack for which it was optimised.
  • The main purpose of wash-out is to prevent tip stall Unloading the tip makes the root stall first, which preserves aileron authority and gives a gentler stall.
  • A strongly twisted wing performs well at every angle of attack

Which measures produce the same effect as an increase of the geometric dihedral angle?

  • Moving the wing from a low to a mid or from a mid to a high position (≈ +3.5° each) Wing position contributes strongly to the effective dihedral — which is why low-wing aircraft need noticeably more geometric dihedral than high-wing aircraft.
  • Adding aft sweep (+10° of sweep ≈ +1° of dihedral) In sideslip the leading wing presents a smaller effective sweep and generates more lift.
  • Adding winglets or cranked wing tips Their primary purpose is vortex drag reduction, but they also add roll stability — sometimes used as a design fix.
  • Adding anhedral

Flight-mechanical consequence of positive effective dihedral: the spiral mode is stabilised, but the Dutch roll mode is destabilised.

How does a positive dihedral angle generate its stabilizing rolling moment?

  • The bank angle causes a sideslip, which increases the angle of attack of the lowered wing and thus its lift The aircraft "slides" towards the lowered wing; the resulting rolling moment tends to level the aircraft again.
  • The lowered wing has more effective area
  • It acts immediately, without any sideslip
  • The vertical tail is pushed sideways and yaws the aircraft level

Which flight situations size the ailerons?

  • Situations with low dynamic pressure, i.e. slow flight The rolling moment scales with q; the slowest required manoeuvre is therefore the critical one.
  • Situations requiring high roll accelerations about the longitudinal axis Typically crosswind landing corrections or, for military aircraft, manoeuvre requirements.
  • High-speed cruise at high altitude
  • Only the deflection angle matters, position and size are irrelevant

Important coupling: the span taken by the ailerons is not available for trailing-edge high-lift devices — and vice versa.

What are spoilers used for?

  • Reducing lift after touchdown or during a rejected take-off Dumping the lift puts weight on the wheels and makes the brakes effective — a major contribution to the braking distance.
  • Increasing drag as an air brake Used for descent and speed control.
  • Roll control by differential deflection Roll spoilers are common on transport jets to augment roll power, especially at low speed.
  • Increasing the maximum lift coefficient for landing

They sit on the upper surface, forward of the flaps and typically aft of the maximum thickness position. A first guess for the design is a position at about 60 % chord.

An aircraft has mTO = 5000 kg, mLanding = 4000 kg, Sref = 25 m² and cruises at 90 m/s at ρ = 0.9 kg/m³. Which design lift coefficient results?

cl,design = mdesign·g / (0.5·ρ·v²·Sref), with mdesign as the average mission mass.

  • ≈ 0.48 mdesign = (5000+4000)/2 = 4500 kg → L = 44 145 N. Dynamic pressure q = 0.5·0.9·90² = 3645 Pa, q·Sref = 91 125 N. cl = 44 145/91 125 ≈ 0.48.
  • ≈ 0.54, using mTO
  • ≈ 1.2
  • ≈ 0.05

The design lift coefficient is the CL the aircraft flies at for most of its mission — the airfoil should have its best L/D exactly there, not just a high L/D somewhere else.

Which statements about high-wing versus low-wing configurations are correct?

  • A high wing gives good downward visibility and an easy, light wing attachment No carry-through structure through the cabin, and the wing box does not eat into the payload volume near the c.g.
  • A low wing shortens the required landing gear length The gear can be attached to the wing close to the ground — one reason why most transport jets are low-wing.
  • A mid wing gives the lowest interference drag The wing-fuselage junction is aerodynamically cleanest — but the carry-through structure sits right where the payload should be.
  • A high wing augments the effective dihedral, a low wing works against it Therefore low-wing aircraft need more geometric dihedral for the same roll stability.
  • A strut-braced wing has particularly low drag

Which functional principles do high-lift devices use?

  • Increase of wing camber The basic effect of any flap: shifting the cl-α curve upwards.
  • Increase of wing area Fowler motion — the flap translates aft before deflecting.
  • Boundary layer control through improved pressure distribution or momentum supply Slots feed high-energy air onto the upper surface; active systems blow or suck.
  • Removal of the "spoiled" boundary layer Also a boundary-layer control mechanism, mainly used in active systems.
  • Reduction of wing area to increase the wing loading

Passive devices (surfaces placed into the flow) are widely used; active devices (suction or blowing) remain rare.

Why are leading-edge devices such as slats usually combined with powerful trailing-edge flaps?

  • Trailing-edge flaps shift the stall to a lower angle of attack; the leading-edge device compensates this shift A plain or slotted flap raises cl but reduces αstall. A slat delays separation and restores (or increases) the stall angle.
  • The combination delivers a considerably higher usable CL,max than either device alone The full benefit of the trailing-edge device can only be exploited if the wing does not stall before the flap's lift increment is reached.
  • Because slats alone produce the largest camber increase
  • Because leading-edge devices reduce the drag of the flapped wing

Careful with the diagrams: the sketched cl-α curves are 2D airfoil data referred to the clean wing chord line and the clean reference area — they must still be corrected for a swept 3D wing.

Raymer's method estimates the maximum lift of a partially flapped wing. Which statements are correct?

CL,max ≈ 0.9 · [ cl,max,flapped·(Sflapped/Sref) + cl,max,unflapped·(Sunflapped/Sref) ]

  • The 2D section values are weighted with their respective area shares This is the core idea: the wing is split into strips, each with its own maximum lift.
  • The factor 0.9 is a crude correction for 3D effects It accounts for the fact that a 3D wing never reaches the 2D value.
  • For a swept wing an additional factor cos(Λc/4) is applied CL,max ≈ 0.9·cos(Λc/4)·Σ cl,max,i·Si/Sref.
  • The lift increment is only obtained on the flapped part of the wing A frequent mistake in exercises is to apply Δcl to the whole wing.
  • The method is accurate enough to replace wind tunnel tests

Watch the reference area when symmetry is exploited: if only the half wing is discretised, the strips must sum to Sref/2.

Which considerations limit the size of the flapped area?

  • Sufficient span must remain for the ailerons to guarantee roll control The classic conflict between the high-lift group and the flight-mechanics group.
  • Thrust gates interrupt the flap system and reduce its effectiveness High-lift devices work best when they are continuous; engine exhaust cut-outs break them up.
  • The flapped area should be kept as small as possible for aerodynamic reasons
  • Blown flaps solve all layout constraints without penalty

Which typical flap deflection angles are used?

  • 20° – 40° for take-off A compromise: enough lift for the field length, but not so much drag that the climb gradient is lost.
  • 60° – 70° for landing Here the extra drag is welcome, because it steepens the approach and shortens the landing roll.
  • Light aircraft often use no flaps at all for take-off Their field performance is usually not flap-limited and the drag penalty would hurt the climb.
  • 75° is the standard take-off setting because it gives maximum lift

Raymer gives ΔCD0,flap = Fflap·(cflap/c)·(Sflapped/Sref)·(δflap − 10°). Which statements are correct?

  • Fflap = 0.0144 for plain flaps and 0.0074 for slotted flaps Slotted flaps produce roughly half the parasite drag increment of a plain flap at the same setting.
  • The induced drag increment follows ΔCDi,flap = kflap²·(ΔCL,flap)²·cos(Λc/4) With kflap = 0.14 for full-span and 0.28 for half-span flaps: a partial-span flap creates a much more distorted lift distribution and therefore more induced drag.
  • The formula is intended for trailing-edge flaps deflected by more than about 20° Below that the linear fit is not valid.
  • High-lift devices only affect the parasite drag

The missed-approach and second-segment climb cases are exactly where these increments matter, because there the aircraft is heavy, slow, and configured.

Which statement best describes the "classic compromise" of wing design that makes high-lift devices necessary?

  • Wings are oversized for cruise but undersized for take-off and landing The wing is sized by the cruise or field-length constraint; without flaps the low-speed lift would demand a far larger wing, which would then be inefficient in cruise.
  • Wings are undersized for cruise but oversized for take-off and landing
  • A single fixed wing geometry is optimal for both cruise and low speed
  • High-lift devices are used mainly because they reduce wing weight

For first estimates, assume that the flap weight is contained in the wing weight.

Which load share should the nose gear of a tricycle landing gear carry?

  • 8 – 15 % of the aircraft weight Too little load means poor nose wheel steering and braking authority; too much load makes the nose gear heavy and rotation difficult.
  • 80 – 90 %
  • 40 – 50 %
  • 1 – 3 %

For comparison: on a taildragger the two main gears sit slightly ahead of the c.g. and carry 80 – 90 % of the weight.

Which are genuine advantages of a tricycle landing gear over a taildragger?

  • Directional stability on the ground A disturbance produces a restoring reaction; on a taildragger the same disturbance tightens the turn — the classic ground loop.
  • Better view from the cockpit and more comfort for passengers The fuselage sits horizontally on the ground.
  • Good behaviour in crosswind landings and stable two-point landings at high angle of attack No tendency to "jump" as with a taildragger two-point landing.
  • Lower landing gear weight
  • Lower drag for a fixed gear

Disadvantage of the tricycle gear: a complex design (steering, dampers) and a large, heavy and expensive nose gear.

Which landing gear configuration fits which application?

  • Single main gear → sailplanes and some high-altitude reconnaissance aircraft Very simple, light and easy to retract into the fuselage — at the price of poor ground stability and control.
  • Bicycle gear → rarely used, mainly military and VTOL aircraft Two main gears on the symmetry axis at roughly equal distance from the c.g., plus outriggers.
  • Quadricycle gear → very heavy cargo or bomber aircraft Allows a low cabin floor for easy loading and needs no cut-outs in the wing.
  • Multi-bogey gear → large transport aircraft Spreads the load over the pavement and adds redundancy.
  • Taildragger gear → modern large transport jets

Single main gear, bicycle and quadricycle share one drawback: rotation is limited, so take-off happens at low α and the take-off run becomes longer.

Why does CS-25 require at least two wheels per main landing gear strut?

  • Redundancy in case of a tyre failure The bogey arrangement enhances safety by providing redundancy — a burst tyre must not end the landing.
  • Distribution of the load over a larger pavement area This is the general reason for multi-bogey gears on large aircraft: the airport pavement classification limits the load per wheel.
  • To reduce the aerodynamic drag of the gear
  • To reduce the landing gear weight

Which statements about shock absorption are correct?

  • The energy of the vertical velocity is absorbed by the shock absorber, the energy of the horizontal velocity by the brakes Two separate energy paths — sizing the gear means sizing both.
  • The oleo shock strut is the most common absorber type An oil-gas strut: the gas acts as spring, the metered orifice as damper.
  • A rigid axle relies solely on the tyre to absorb the shock Used on light sailplanes, where landing energies are small.
  • Solid spring, levered bungee and triangulated gears all tend to cause tyre scrubbing The wheel moves sideways or fore-aft during deflection, which wears the tyres.
  • A trailing link gear is unsuitable for rough field operations

The landing gear weight is to be distributed between nose and main gear. Which shares are typical?

  • General aviation: 75 – 80 % main gear, 20 – 25 % nose gear Small aircraft have a relatively heavier nose gear share.
  • Large transport: 85 – 92 % main gear, 8 – 15 % nose gear Same trend as the load distribution: the bigger the aircraft, the more dominant the main gear.
  • Fighters: 50 % main gear, 50 % nose gear
  • Military transports: 60 % main gear, 40 % nose gear

The split matters because most Class I and Class II methods only deliver the total gear weight, while the c.g. calculation needs the individual positions.

Gear drag is estimated from frontal area times a drag coefficient. Which values are correct?

  • Regular wheel and tyre: CD ≈ 0.25 The baseline value; a second wheel in tandem only adds about 0.15 because it sits in the wake of the first.
  • Wheel and tyre with fairing: CD ≈ 0.13 Roughly half the drag of the bare wheel — which is why fixed-gear aircraft use wheel pants.
  • Flat spring gear leg: CD ≈ 1.4 A bluff body in cross-flow — one of the worst offenders, comparable to a fork or bogey fitting.
  • Streamline strut: CD ≈ 0.05 Compared with about 0.30 for a round strut or wire — a factor of six for the same structural job.
  • Round strut or wire: CD ≈ 0.02

Gear drag matters most for the missed-approach L/D of a retractable-gear aircraft and for the whole cruise of a fixed-gear aircraft.

At which point of the design process can the "final" position of the landing gear be determined?

  • Only after the empennage layout and sizing (Chapter 11) is complete The gear position depends on the c.g. — and the c.g. position (and the wing position relative to the fuselage) is only fixed by the stability and control analysis.
  • Immediately in Chapter 10, since the design rules are purely geometric
  • Right at the end of the whole design process, after the three-view
  • Already during preliminary sizing (Chapter 5)

Because the gear is one of the heaviest single systems, shifting it has a large effect on the c.g. — which is why gear position and c.g. must be iterated together.

What is the purpose of the empennage and its control surfaces?

  • To provide trim Equilibrium of moments about the c.g. must be achievable for every permissible combination of c.g. position, flap setting, speed and engine condition.
  • To provide stability The tendency to return to the original flight state after a disturbance.
  • To provide control Enough control power for the required manoeuvrability, without excessive control forces.
  • To generate the main part of the lift

All three tasks are realised by generating aerodynamic moments about the total centre of gravity.

How are the tail volume coefficients defined?

  • CH = (SH/SW) · (lH/cMAC) The horizontal tail is referenced to the mean aerodynamic chord, because it works about the pitch axis.
  • CV = (SV/SW) · (lV/b) The vertical tail is referenced to the wing span, because it works about the yaw axis.
  • CH = (SH/SW) · (lH/b)
  • CV = SV · lV, with the dimension of a volume

lH and lV are the distances from the neutral point of the respective stabilizer to the total aircraft c.g. The tail volume is a measure of the effectiveness of a stabilizer with respect to both its size and its position.

Two designs have the same horizontal tail volume coefficient, but design B has a 20 % longer tail lever arm. What follows?

  • Design B needs a roughly 17 % smaller horizontal tail area SH ~ 1/lH for constant CH: a factor 1/1.2 ≈ 0.83.
  • Design B saves structural weight and drag in the tail Exactly the reason for the design rule "extend the lever arms as far as the available fuselage allows".
  • Design B has a much less effective tail
  • The longer lever arm always makes the aircraft heavier overall

The limit is the available fuselage length and the resulting structural and c.g. consequences.

Which design rules apply to stabilizer surfaces?

  • Use symmetric airfoils to keep trim changes with speed small A cambered stabilizer would produce a speed-dependent moment that constantly needs re-trimming.
  • Avoid placing the stabilizer in the propeller or jet stream Reasons: induced vibrations and structural fatigue, cabin noise, trim changes with engine setting, and hot jet plumes damaging the structure.
  • The stabilizer must stall well after the main wing Controllability must be ensured at all times — achieved by low aspect ratio, a gently stalling airfoil, and a higher critical Mach number.
  • For transonic aircraft: sweep about 5° higher and t/c about 10 % lower than the outboard wing This raises the critical Mach number of the tail by roughly ΔMacrit = 0.05 relative to the wing.
  • The rule about the propeller stream has no exceptions

Which sequence describes the typical empennage design procedure?

The answers refer to the order of the steps, so they are not shuffled.

  1. Configuration decision → sizing via tail volume coefficients → initial wing position guess → final sizing from trim and stability → iterate if the deviation exceeds 10 % The statistical estimate provides the starting point; the stability and control analysis provides the real answer; the loop is closed if they disagree by more than about 10 %.
  2. Trim and stability analysis first, then the wing position, then the tail volume estimate
  3. Tail volume coefficients only; a further analysis is unnecessary
  4. Accept the result of the trim and stability analysis regardless of how far it deviates from the statistical estimate

If the calculated tail areas come out considerably smaller than the statistical values, be suspicious: dynamic stability, which is not analysed in conceptual design, may well be driving the real tail size.

Which flight states are critical for the sizing of the horizontal stabilizer?

  • The critical combination of c.g. position, flap setting and speed Typically the most forward c.g. with maximum flap deflection in the landing configuration.
  • Take-off rotation The tail must be able to rotate the aircraft about the main gear at VR.
  • One engine inoperative on a multi-engine aircraft
  • Spin recovery

Depending on the engine position, the sizing case differs: for engines below the c.g. the flare in landing configuration with the most forward c.g. is critical; for engines above the c.g. it is the go-around.

Which flight states are critical for the sizing of the vertical stabilizer?

  • Engine failure on a multi-engine aircraft Usually the design case: the yawing moment from the asymmetric thrust plus the drag of the dead engine must be balanced down to VMC.
  • Take-off and landing in crosswind The rudder must be able to align the aircraft with the runway.
  • Spin recovery Requires unblanketed rudder area — at least about one third of the rudder should stay in clean flow.
  • Symmetric cruise flight at maximum speed

The vertical stabilizer is sized for one engine inoperative. Which contributions make up the yawing moment to be balanced?

  • The thrust moment of the operating engine, NE = Ttake-off · yE Asymmetric thrust times the lateral engine offset.
  • The drag moment of the failed engine, ND Depends strongly on the engine type: windmilling fixed-pitch propeller ≈ 0.8·blade area·yE·q, feathered variable-pitch propeller ≈ 0.1·blade area·yE·q, jet engine ≈ 0.3·frontal area·yE·q.
  • The rolling moment due to the bank angle
  • The case is uncritical because it occurs at high speed

SV = (NE + ND) / (½·ρ·vMC²·lV·cY,V), where cY,V is the maximum side force coefficient including rudder deflection.

A feathered propeller produces roughly an eighth of the drag moment of a windmilling one — which is why feathering is such a decisive safety feature.

What are dorsal and ventral fins used for?

  • A dorsal fin increases directional stability at high sideslip angles The vortex forming on the fin delays the fin stall, and the added area helps spin prevention and recovery.
  • A ventral fin increases directional stability at high angles of attack When the vertical tail is blanketed by the fuselage, the ventral fin still sees clean airflow — very useful for spin prevention and recovery.
  • Both are used to deliberately reduce directional stability
  • Both mainly increase roll stability

Which statements about the position of the horizontal tail relative to the wing wake are correct?

  • At high angles of attack, but before the stall, the wake hitting the tail is desirable as a natural stall warning The resulting buffet and stick shake tells the pilot that the stall is approaching.
  • In the fully stalled condition the tail must not be immersed in the wing wake Otherwise the stabilizer loses effectiveness exactly when it is needed, which can lead to an unrecoverable pitch-up — the deep stall.
  • The position of the horizontal tail relative to the vertical tail matters for spin recovery The horizontal tail must not blanket the rudder; at least about one third of the rudder area should remain in clean flow.
  • The wing wake has no relevant effect on stabilizer efficiency

Which guidelines apply to elevator and rudder sizing in conceptual design?

  • They usually extend from close to the root over about 90 – 100 % of the stabilizer half span Unlike ailerons, control surfaces on the empennage normally use nearly the whole span.
  • The chord ratio is chosen from statistical guidelines at this stage The exact chord follows only from a detailed flight-mechanical analysis in later phases.
  • Statistical values are sufficient; no further analysis is required
  • Large aircraft trim exclusively with trim tabs

What is plotted in a "scissor" diagram?

  • The stabilizer area ratio SH/SW over the c.g. position (relative to the wing aerodynamic centre) Both the trim condition and the static stability condition can be expressed as lines in this plane — their shape gives the diagram its name.
  • Thrust-to-weight ratio over wing loading
  • Payload over range
  • Lift coefficient over drag coefficient

It answers two questions simultaneously: how large must the horizontal tail be, and which c.g. range is permissible for a given tail size.

Which line in the scissor diagram comes from which requirement?

  • The static stability condition gives a straight line through the origin, SH/SW ≈ a · xCG-AC It follows from setting CM,α,CG = 0 and defines the aft c.g. limit: the further aft the c.g., the more tail area is needed.
  • The trim condition gives a line with an additional constant term from CM,W and CM,E SH/SW = CL,W/(CL,H·ηH·lH/cMAC)·xCG-AC + (CM,W+CM,E)/(CL,H·ηH·lH/cMAC). It defines the forward c.g. limit.
  • The two lines together enclose the permissible combinations of tail area and c.g. position For a chosen SH/SW the horizontal cut between the two lines gives the allowable c.g. range.
  • Only the stability line has to be respected; the trim line is optional

The intersection of the two lines marks the smallest theoretically possible horizontal tail area — at which, however, the permissible c.g. range shrinks to a single point.

The slope of the stability line is a = CL,α,W / [ CL,α,H · ηH · (1 − dε/dα) · lH/cMAC ]. Which parameters therefore influence the scissor diagram?

  • The tail lever arm lH and the mean aerodynamic chord cMAC A longer lever arm flattens the stability line, so less tail area is needed for the same c.g. position.
  • The downwash gradient dε/dα A strong downwash gradient reduces the effective angle-of-attack change at the tail and therefore its stabilizing contribution — the required area grows.
  • The dynamic pressure ratio ηH A tail in a strongly disturbed flow is less effective and needs more area.
  • The ratio of the lift curve slopes of wing and tail A wing with a high CL,α is more destabilising for an aft c.g., a tail with a high CL,α is more effective.
  • The wing loading W/S

Additional parameters entering the trim line: the zero-lift pitching moment of the wing CM,W (and hence the airfoil camber and flap setting), the engine moment CM,E, and the maximum usable tail lift coefficient CL,H.

A required static margin is introduced instead of using the neutral point directly. What happens in the scissor diagram?

  • The stability line shifts, so that a given tail area allows a c.g. only further forward CM,α,CG = −CL,α·(static margin): demanding a genuinely negative CM,α instead of just zero moves the aft c.g. limit forward.
  • For an unchanged c.g. range, a larger horizontal tail is required Stability margin has to be paid for with tail area, weight and drag.
  • The condition CM,α,CG = 0 is the design goal for a stable aircraft
  • The static margin only affects the forward c.g. limit

Modern fighters deliberately go the other way: relaxed static stability with a c.g. behind the neutral point, made flyable by a computerised flight control system, and rewarded with a much smaller tail and less trim drag.

During the design the flap deflection for landing is increased, which makes the wing pitching moment CM,W considerably more negative. What is the effect?

  • The trim line shifts, and a larger horizontal tail is required for the same forward c.g. The nose-down moment of the flapped wing has to be balanced by a larger tail download — the trim case, not the stability case, becomes more demanding.
  • The permissible forward c.g. limit moves aft for an unchanged tail area The trim line is the forward boundary of the usable c.g. range.
  • The slope of the stability line changes
  • Flap setting is irrelevant for empennage sizing

This coupling is one reason why high-lift design (Chapter 9) and empennage sizing (Chapter 11) have to be iterated together.

Which condition describes longitudinal static stability?

  • CM,α,CG = ∂CM,CG/∂α < 0 An increase in angle of attack must produce a nose-down moment, which drives the aircraft back to its original state.
  • ∂CM,CG/∂α > 0
  • ∂CM,CG/∂α = 0
  • CM,CG = 0 at every angle of attack

Static stability means all moments generated in the disturbed position tend to restore the original flight state. Dynamic stability additionally requires that the resulting oscillation is damped.

Static stability is only a necessary, not a sufficient condition for dynamic stability.

How do the individual contributions to longitudinal stability act?

  • The wing is destabilising when the c.g. lies behind the aerodynamic centre The term CL,α,W·xCG-AC is positive for an aft c.g., pushing CM,α towards instability.
  • The horizontal stabilizer is stabilising Its lift increment acts at a long lever arm behind the c.g. and produces a nose-down moment when α increases.
  • The downwash of the wing reduces the tail contribution by the factor (1 − dε/dα) The tail only feels part of the free-stream angle-of-attack change.
  • The fuselage is strongly stabilising in pitch

Full expression: CM,α,CG = CL,α,W·xCG-AC − CL,α,H·ηH·(SH/SW)·(1 − dε/dα)·(lH/cMAC − xCG-AC)

Which statements about lateral-directional static stability are correct?

  • Directional static stability requires that a positive sideslip angle β produces a positive yawing moment about the c.g. CN,β > 0 — the aircraft turns into the relative wind, like a weathervane.
  • The fuselage contribution is usually destabilising CN,β,F < 0, which is precisely what the vertical stabilizer has to overcome.
  • The wing contribution can be neglected for small sideslip angles A simplification used in the conceptual sizing of the fin.
  • The vertical stabilizer contributes destabilising yawing moments

Solving the moment balance for the fin area gives SV/SW = [(CN,β − CN,β,F)/(−CY,β,V)] · (bW/lV).

An aircraft has a strong effective dihedral (high wing plus aft sweep plus geometric dihedral). Which consequences follow?

  • The spiral mode is stabilised The strong roll-due-to-sideslip effect levels the aircraft again.
  • The Dutch roll mode is destabilised The same effect couples roll and yaw and reduces the damping of the Dutch roll oscillation.
  • Anhedral may be required as a design fix Which is exactly why many high-wing, swept transports (military airlifters) show noticeable anhedral.
  • The longitudinal static stability is improved

This is a good example of why "more stability" is not automatically better: the two lateral modes have opposite preferences.

What does the "loading envelope" describe and why does it matter?

  • The permissible combinations of take-off weight and c.g. position Depending on payload, fuel load and loading arrangement, the aircraft must operate safely over a whole range of weights and c.g. positions.
  • It defines the c.g. range that the empennage sizing must cover The tail must trim the most forward and remain stable at the most aft c.g. of the whole envelope, not just at one nominal point.
  • It later becomes the basis for the load and trim sheet of the operator The operational document that ensures each individual flight stays inside the certified envelope.
  • It is enough to size the empennage for a single design c.g. position

The drag polar is written as CD = CD0 + (CL − CL0)²/(π·A·e). Which statements are correct?

  • CD0 is the lift-independent (zero-lift) drag Skin friction plus form drag plus miscellaneous contributions.
  • The second term is the lift-dependent (induced) drag It grows with the square of the lift coefficient — which is why induced drag dominates at low speed and parasite drag at high speed.
  • A cambered airfoil shifts the minimum of the polar to CL0 ≠ 0 The polar is no longer symmetric about CL = 0; the aircraft has minimum drag at a positive lift coefficient.
  • Increasing the aspect ratio A reduces the induced drag at a given CL A appears in the denominator.
  • e is always exactly 1

Which condition holds at the point of maximum aerodynamic efficiency (L/D)max?

  • The induced drag equals the zero-lift drag Setting d(CD/CL)/dCL = 0 gives CDi = CD0 — half of the total drag is induced, half is parasite.
  • The corresponding lift coefficient is CL = √(π·A·e·CD0) Directly from equating the two drag contributions.
  • The maximum efficiency is (L/D)max = ½·√(π·A·e/CD0) This single formula shows the two levers: increase the aspect ratio, or reduce the zero-lift drag.
  • It occurs at the maximum lift coefficient
  • It occurs at zero lift, where the drag is smallest

Geometrically, (L/D)max is the point where a straight line from the origin is tangent to the drag polar.

How does the component build-up method determine the zero-lift drag?

CD0 = Σ (Cf,i · FFi · Qi · Swet,i) / Sref + CD,misc

  • Each component is represented by a flat plate with the same wetted area and Reynolds number This gives the flat-plate skin friction coefficient Cf.
  • The form factor FF accounts for the drag due to the actual shape Pressure drag caused by thickness — it depends on t/c or fineness ratio and on Mach number.
  • The factor Q accounts for interference drag The mutual influence of touching bodies; it is highly complex and is therefore approximated statistically.
  • Miscellaneous drag must be added separately for flaps, gear, engine installation and cooling Engine installation and cooling drag, for instance, may be estimated at around 10 % of the total CD0.
  • Small components can be neglected without consequence

Which aerodynamic analysis methods are suitable for drag estimation in conceptual design?

  • Handbook (build-up) methods such as DATCOM Quick, reasonably accurate, easily implemented in a spreadsheet — and still the method of choice for drag polars in early design.
  • RANS methods Suited for drag, but with computation times of hours per case they are used for spot-checks rather than for design sweeps.
  • DES / LES methods Physically suitable, but days to weeks of computing time make them impractical in conceptual design.
  • Vortex lattice / full potential methods
  • Euler methods

The general trade-off is between accuracy and computation time. Because shear forces on the wetted surfaces and locally confined flow features dominate the drag build-up, handbook methods remain competitive.

Which measures actually improve the aerodynamic performance (L/D) of an aircraft?

  • Increasing the aspect ratio Reduces induced drag; the price is structural weight, so a trade study is needed.
  • Reducing the wetted area relative to the reference area CD0 scales directly with Swet/Sref — this is the fundamental argument behind blended and flying-wing configurations.
  • Winglets or well-designed wing tips They primarily reduce vortex drag; as a side effect they add roll stability.
  • Promoting laminar flow, e.g. with 6-series or natural laminar flow airfoils A laminar boundary layer produces far less skin friction — but the benefit is fragile (surface quality, contamination, Reynolds number).
  • Reducing interference drag at wing-fuselage and tail-fuselage junctions Fairings and fillets attack the Q factor of the build-up method.
  • Choosing the wing position with the highest interference drag
Be sceptical about unproven drag-saving technology — new winglets, laminar flow control and similar promises have disappointed even experienced companies.

Which statements about the critical Mach number and the drag divergence Mach number are correct?

  • At Ma* the local flow first reaches Ma = 1 somewhere on the airfoil This is the definition of the critical Mach number; from there on regions of supersonic flow develop.
  • MaDD > Ma* The strong drag rise does not start at Ma* but somewhat above it, once the shocks have become strong enough.
  • Ma* depends on airfoil shape, thickness ratio and sweep angle The three main design levers for transonic wing design.
  • Supercritical airfoils are used to increase Ma* They spread the suction over the upper surface so that a higher free-stream Mach number can be flown before strong shocks form.
  • Above MaDD the drag decreases again

A further transonic problem is buffet: shock oscillations that excite the structure (buffeting) and cause fatigue.

What does the area rule state?

  • At transonic and supersonic speeds the wave drag of a wing-body combination is similar to that of a body of revolution with the same cross-sectional area distribution Hence the "coke-bottle" fuselage waisting where the wing joins.
  • The minimum wave drag is achieved for a shape as close as possible to the Sears-Haack body The smooth area distribution of the Sears-Haack body is the theoretical optimum.
  • The area rule minimises the skin friction drag
  • Wave drag can be predicted very accurately with simple handbook formulas

Which drag contributions belong to which category?

  • Skin friction and form drag: lift-independent Together they make up the bulk of CD0 in subsonic flight.
  • Induced (vortex) drag: lift-dependent, caused by 3D effects CDi = CL²/(πAe). It does not exist in 2D flow — which is why airfoils can reach L/D values above 100.
  • Trim drag: caused by the lift the tailplane must produce to trim the aircraft Partly induced drag, partly profile drag from the control surface deflection; it can only be determined together with the flight-mechanical analysis.
  • Interference drag: additional drag from the flow around touching bodies Treated statistically through the factor Q.
  • Wave drag: always present, even in low-speed flight

Why can a 2D airfoil reach L/D values above 100, while a complete aircraft rarely exceeds about 20?

  • There is no induced drag in 2D flow Induced drag is a consequence of the finite span and the resulting tip vortices — the dominant penalty of a real wing at high lift.
  • The complete aircraft adds fuselage, empennage, nacelles, gear and interference drag Every additional component adds wetted area and therefore parasite drag, without adding lift.
  • The parabolic shape of the 2D drag polar comes only from separation at higher angles of attack Not from induced drag, as is often wrongly assumed.
  • Because a 2D airfoil has no skin friction drag

Consequence for design: the required 2D airfoil L/D depends heavily on the 3D wing geometry — determining it is not a trivial task.

Two designs for the same mission are compared. Design A has CD0 = 0.020 and A·e = 12; design B has CD0 = 0.025 and A·e = 16. Which statements are correct?

(L/D)max = ½·√(π·A·e/CD0)

  • Design B has the higher maximum aerodynamic efficiency A: ½·√(π·12/0.020) = ½·√1885 ≈ 21.7. B: ½·√(π·16/0.025) = ½·√2011 ≈ 22.4.
  • A low CD0 alone is not a measure of aerodynamic quality Only the combination of CD0 and A·e determines (L/D)max. Here the higher aspect ratio more than compensates the higher zero-lift drag.
  • Both designs have exactly the same (L/D)max
  • Design A wins, because a lower CD0 is always better
  • The comparison of (L/D)max is sufficient to pick the better design

Both effects also carry weight penalties in opposite directions, so the final choice still needs a full sizing loop.

Which conclusions should be drawn from the "lessons learned" on weight and drag prediction?

  • Under-predicting drag has the same consequences as under-predicting weight Both destroy the performance guarantees on which the whole project was sold.
  • Claims about new, unproven technology deserve serious scepticism New winglets, laminar flow control, VTOL technology, composite structures, box-wing configurations — all classic sources of optimistic predictions.
  • Estimates should be cross-checked with a second, independent method Especially when the design deviates significantly from the reference aircraft the statistics were derived from.
  • Experienced companies are immune to optimistic predictions
